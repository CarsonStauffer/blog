Postmortem

August 27, 2020

So...where are Startups #3 and #4? I didn't do them. I quit the challenge, which is ironic given that one of my goals was to practice follow through. I guess that continues to be one of my weaknesses.

I failed to make 4 startups. Technically, I failed the challenge. However, I don't consider the experience a failure.

I did create 2 startups. While they were amateur and I question if they each merited spending a full month of my time, I learned a lot from them.

What went poorly

Finishing, of course. The results of the first two projects were discouraging, and my motivation dwindled as the months wore on. Eventually, I found something I believed to be a better use of my time and honestly felt relieved when I moved on.

Fulfilling my vision. When I imagined these products, I thought of big picture ideas, and let the details stay blurry. Like the way your brain remembers a picture. For example, with Backpackingcost, I got the math working easily, but had no idea how to turn it into a sleek, appealing site that looked professional. When imagining the site, I didn't give any thought to design. I assumed it would come automatically. When I arrived at the end of the month, and it looked terribly amateur, I was disappointed.

What went well

Learning. I learned a lot from the startups I created. They helped me to understand gaps in my expertise and aspects about my personality that make this type of work challenging. They also showed me I have a long way to go before I'm capable of creating anything worth people's time. That doesn't mean I can't get there, but I have some work to do.

One Year Later

I quit working on this challenge because I had an opportunity to make games again with my longtime friend Patrick. We collaborated on a game together after college in 2015-16. We were proud to finish that game, but it was a commercial failure, ultimately due to our lack of experience.

We were pretty burnt out on games after Kitty Lander, but 3 years was enough time to lick our wounds and rekindle our interest in games. We were better prepared this time around, but it still wasn't enough. We fell into many of the same traps as last time (no knowledge or desire to do marketing, limited art and design abilities, making a game that people just didn't want to play) and found ourselves with another flop.

I would consider this game to be the spiritual successor to Startups #1 and #2. It was another self-inflicted project, only larger.

I never said it, but the implied understanding is that these startups could eventually generate some sort of income and merit further investment. After a year and a half of "working" on my own (read: making half-finished games and hobby projects) and not earning a dollar, I'm here to throw in the towel.

This has been a humbling experience. It began 5 years ago with Kitty Lander, and while I took a break with over 2 years of "actual" work and travel, it has continued until this day. 5 years ago, I was 23 years old, fresh out of school, and cocky as hell. I thought I was too good to work for the man. I wanted to take the shortcut. If all it took to be the next big indie developer was sitting down and making a game, then that's exactly what I was going to do!

It never occurred to me that getting a job in the industry would have been a crash course in how to develop games successfully. I could have worked alongside people that have mastered making games. People who are smarter and more skilled at it than I ever hope to be. And I would have been paid to be there.

Now I sit here, 5 years later, only to find out that the shortcut led nowhere. Now I must march back to where I started, years older with little to show for it. Ironically, taking a job would have been the shortcut. Even if I had worked 3 years in the industry, I would have had time to leave afterwards and create the games that I did (and they probably would have been a whole lot better too).

The Future

Experiencing so much failure has been hard, but I'm staying optimistic. I'm grateful to have learned the lessons that I did. I've had some amazing experiences in these last few years that wouldn't have been possible if I was just busting my ass at my career.

One day, I think it would be fun to work on my own games again. Or even other creations. But not for a long time. I want to be around other productive people. I want to feel some payoff for the work I am doing.

I still love to create things. I like to let my imagination run wild and make things people enjoy. And now I understand I can feel that joy even if I am working on someone else's idea. I'm looking for work now, and I have never been more excited about it.
